The Belonging Gap: Why School Size Shapes Student Success

# The Belonging Gap: Why School Size Shapes Student Success More Than Most Report Cards Show

For a lot of high schoolers, the most grinding part of the day isn't a hard test or a tough teacher. It's walking through a crowd of a thousand people and feeling completely invisible.

Education researchers have a name for this: the belonging gap. And in 2025, it sits quietly behind two of the most urgent crises in K-12 education — a stubborn epidemic of chronic absenteeism and a continued decline in student mental health. Schools are pouring resources into tutoring programs, mental health counselors, and attendance alert systems. Many of those efforts are worthwhile. But a growing body of research suggests they're all fighting uphill if students don't first feel like they genuinely belong at school.

---

What the Belonging Gap Actually Means

School belonging isn't soft language for "kids feeling happy." It's a well-defined construct in education psychology — measuring whether students feel accepted, respected, valued, and supported by the people in their school environment. And the research backing its importance is substantial.

A 2024 systematic literature review published in the European Journal of Investigating Health, Psychology and Education, examining 86 studies conducted between 1990 and 2023, confirmed that the sense of school belonging plays an important role in students' academic, behavioral, and psychological outcomes. The effects aren't marginal. A separate 2025 study found that higher levels of school belonging are associated with lower mental health symptoms across adulthood — meaning what students feel in ninth grade echoes decades later.

The attendance link is equally direct. Research consistently identifies student disengagement and lack of connectedness as significant drivers of chronic absenteeism. One study surveying nearly 600 public high school students found that positive perceptions of the school environment — including clear rules, a sense of belonging, and positive adult relationships — were associated with lower rates of absenteeism. Belonging isn't a nice-to-have. It's load-bearing infrastructure.

---

The Numbers Are Hard to Ignore

The gap between students who feel connected and those who don't is measurable — and it maps cleanly onto school size.

According to survey data from the nonprofit YouthTruth, 51% of students at small schools reported feeling like part of their school community, compared to just 39% of students at large schools. That 12-point gap represents hundreds of thousands of students who go through a school day feeling peripheral to it.

The overall picture isn't encouraging either. Only 42% of middle and high school students reported feeling like part of their school community in recent YouthTruth data. Meanwhile, just 22% of students said their teachers made an effort to understand their life outside of school — down sharply from 43% before the COVID-19 pandemic. In the years since, mental health has become a greater obstacle to learning, not a lesser one, with nearly half of secondary students reporting that mental health impedes their ability to do their best at school.

Chronic absenteeism compounds all of this. The U.S. Department of Education defines chronic absenteeism as missing 10% or more of school — roughly one day every two weeks. In 2023, the national chronic absenteeism rate hit 28%. In some states, it climbed higher still. And in interviews, district leaders described a cultural shift in which more students and families have come to see school as optional — a problem that early-warning software alone cannot fix.

---

Why Belonging Has to Be Built Into School Design

When researchers ask why students disengage, the answers keep circling back to the same themes: they don't feel safe (emotionally or physically), they don't feel known by adults, and they don't see the point. Protective factors that improve attendance — receiving support from a teacher or staff member, having a positive growth mindset, feeling connected to school climate — are all relational. They require adults who have enough time and proximity to actually deliver them.

That's where structure matters. A teacher managing 150 students across six periods has limited bandwidth to notice the sophomore who's started sitting in the back corner and turning in fewer assignments. A teacher who sees the same 22 students daily in a smaller setting has a fundamentally different opportunity.

Research consistently shows that smaller class sizes allow educators to provide more individualized attention, resulting in stronger teacher-student relationships and better student outcomes. Smaller class sizes in charter schools are also correlated with fewer discipline problems — another indirect marker of belonging, since students who feel connected to their community are less likely to act against it.

---

Where Charter Schools Have a Structural Advantage

This is precisely where mission-driven charter schools have a real edge — not because they're inherently better, but because their design often creates the conditions belonging requires.

Charter school enrollment has grown by more than 15% over the last six years, adding over half a million students even as the overall school-age population shrinks. In the most recent year of data, charter schools added more than 111,000 students while district schools lost more than 221,000. The National Alliance for Public Charter Schools' 2025 Enrollment Brief found that 39 out of 43 state charter sectors saw growth over the last year. Families are voting with their enrollment decisions — and the reasons they cite consistently include personalized learning, clear school culture, and the sense that their child will actually be known.

Career Path High, a tuition-free charter school serving grades 9–12 in Kaysville, Utah, is an example of the kind of smaller, focused environment where that belonging isn't left to chance. In a setting built around a defined mission rather than a default attendance zone, the structural conditions for connection are woven in from the start.

---

What "Designing for Belonging" Looks Like in Practice

Schools — charter and traditional alike — that are closing the belonging gap tend to share a set of intentional practices:

  • Named advisory relationships. Every student has one adult who is explicitly responsible for knowing them — not just their GPA, but their circumstances.
  • Cohort-based scheduling. Students move through parts of the day with consistent small groups, building peer relationships that stick.
  • Mission clarity. When a school stands for something specific, students opt in to an identity — and that ownership matters.
  • Proactive absence response. Rather than sending automated letters after ten days, staff reach out after the first unexplained absence. Early intervention, not bureaucratic escalation.
  • Social-emotional learning integrated into instruction. SEL supports are particularly important for students who have experienced trauma or stress, enabling academic tenacity and resilience alongside cognitive skills.

The difference between a school that talks about community and one that structurally enables it often comes down to whether adults have the time and the mandate to act on what they observe.

---

The Question Worth Asking Before Enrollment

If you're a parent evaluating schools — or an educator designing one — the most predictive question isn't about test scores or AP course counts. It's simpler: What happens when a student starts to pull away?

In a school where belonging is architected into the daily structure, the answer is fast and personal: someone notices, someone reaches out, and the student is reoriented before a few low days become a semester of disconnection. In a school where belonging is incidental, the answer tends to arrive in a letter, weeks too late.

The belonging gap is real, it's measurable, and it's one of the clearest arguments for thinking carefully about school design — not just school funding — when we talk about student success. Smaller schools and mission-driven environments don't guarantee belonging. But they make it structurally possible in ways that large, anonymous institutions simply can't replicate at scale.

Every student deserves to walk into a building and feel like the place would notice their absence — for the right reasons, before it becomes a pattern.
