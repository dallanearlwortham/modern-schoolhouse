What Makes Charter Schools Work? The Research

# What Actually Makes Charter Schools Work? The Research Behind the Results

Most conversations about charter schools get stuck on the wrong question. Do charters outperform traditional public schools? The short answer, based on the best available national research, is increasingly yes — on average. The more useful question — the one that actually matters for students and families — is why the strongest charter schools produce the results they do, and what separates them from those that don't.

That question now has real, research-backed answers.

The Data Baseline: Charter Schools and Student Learning Gains

The most rigorous national snapshot comes from Stanford University's Center for Research on Education Outcomes (CREDO), which released its third National Charter School Study in 2023 — the most extensive in the project's 15-year history. Researchers analyzed year-over-year academic progress for students across thousands of charter schools in 29 states, the District of Columbia, and New York City.

Using a standard 180-day school year as a benchmark, the study found that charter school students gained an average of 16 additional days of learning in reading and 6 additional days in math compared to peers in traditional public schools. Overall, 36% of charter school students showed stronger growth than their traditional public school peers, 47% made similar progress, and 17% showed weaker results.

Those numbers aren't a blanket endorsement of every charter school — they're a national average. And that's exactly the point. The variation within the charter sector is where the real story lives.

Who Benefits Most — and Who Doesn't

The CREDO research found that Black and Hispanic charter school students show the largest gains, advancing more than their traditional public school peers by large margins in both math and reading. English learners and students living in poverty also showed stronger growth in charter settings — often the very students who face the greatest barriers when assigned to a struggling neighborhood school.

The picture is not uniformly positive, however. Students receiving special education services showed markedly weaker performance in charter schools compared to their traditional public school peers — a consistent finding across the research that the charter sector cannot afford to ignore as it continues to grow.

Nationally, approximately 3.7 million students now attend charter schools, making up roughly 7.6% of all public school students. Charter enrollment continues to grow even as overall school-age population declines — a signal that families are actively seeking out alternatives, not simply defaulting to them.

What Separates High-Performing Charters from the Rest

This is the question that matters most: not whether charters beat district schools on average, but what specific practices drive results in the ones that do.

Research from Harvard economists Will Dobbie and Roland Fryer, Jr., examining charter schools in New York City, identified five factors most predictive of a charter school's success:

  • Frequent teacher feedback — consistent, specific instructional coaching rather than once-a-year evaluations
  • Data-driven instruction — using real-time student performance data to adjust teaching, not just to report outcomes after the fact
  • High-dosage tutoring — targeted, small-group or individual academic support built into the regular school day
  • Increased instructional time — longer school days or extended school years that create more time on task
  • High expectations — a school-wide culture where rigorous academic achievement is the norm, not the exception

These practices tend to be embraced by many of the best-known charter networks, including KIPP, Success Academy, and Achievement First. Notably, emerging research suggests that the benefits of charter attendance extend beyond test scores — studies have found positive relationships between charter enrollment and long-term outcomes including college attendance and reduced involvement in the criminal justice system.

Accountability Is Part of Why the Sector Is Improving

One underappreciated driver of the charter sector's overall improvement over time is accountability-driven closure of low-performing schools. A landmark CREDO study found that at least 300 low-performing charter schools closed between 2006 and 2012 — a factor that likely contributed to measurable sector-wide gains during that period.

In Ohio, charters' impact on student achievement went from negative to positive after a 2015 policy change that ramped up accountability requirements on charter authorizers and led to dozens of school closures. The lesson is counterintuitive but consistent across states: a rigorous authorizing environment doesn't weaken the charter sector — it strengthens it by removing schools that aren't delivering and reinforcing the conditions that allow effective schools to thrive.

This accountability-quality connection is exactly the kind of systemic work that state-level charter organizations make possible. The Public Charter School Alliance of South Carolina serves as South Carolina's statewide voice for public charter schools, working to ensure that every student in the state has access to a high-quality public school. Beyond advocacy, the Alliance supports member schools with best-practice resources, connects families to a public charter school directory, and helps build the policy and operational conditions under which strong charter schools can grow — and underperforming ones can be held to account.

The Honest Limits of the Research

Do all charter schools automatically outperform district schools? No. The research is explicit that outcomes vary widely by state, region, school network, and grade level. Not every charter school outperforms its district counterparts, and some struggle with performance challenges that trigger accountability measures or, ultimately, closure.

What the best research does support is this: when charter schools adopt evidence-based instructional practices, maintain high expectations, and operate within a strong accountability framework, they produce meaningful, measurable learning gains — particularly for students who have historically been underserved by traditional district-assigned schools.

What Families Should Actually Ask When Choosing a Charter School

If you're weighing a charter school for your child, aggregate national data is a starting point — not a decision. The questions most directly grounded in the research are:

  • How does the school use student data to adjust instruction from week to week?
  • What does tutoring or academic intervention look like for students who are falling behind?
  • How does school leadership give teachers regular, specific feedback on their classroom practice?
  • What are the school's documented outcomes for students with learning differences or IEPs?
  • Who authorizes this school, and what does the renewal and accountability process look like?

Schools with honest, specific answers to those questions — not just polished mission statements — are the ones most likely to be putting the research into practice.

---

The debate over whether charter schools "work" is largely settled at the national level. The more important conversation — the one that will actually shape student outcomes — is about how they work, and whether the instructional practices behind the strongest results are reaching more schools, more classrooms, and more kids.
