Who Fights for Charter Schools When No One's Watching?

# Who Fights for Charter Schools When No One's Watching?

North Carolina's charter sector just hit a historic milestone: 213 schools serving over 161,000 students in 2024–25, with charter students now accounting for roughly 10.5% of all public school enrollment in the state. Waitlists at 138 of those schools top 59,000 names. The demand is unmistakable.

But when families finally land a spot at a charter school that fits their child—one that maybe opens at 7 a.m. for working parents, or runs a project-based curriculum their kid actually thrives in—they're not thinking about the policy fights that made that school possible. They're thinking about backpacks and bus stops.

Behind those celebrated enrollment numbers is an infrastructure most people never see: the state charter school associations doing the unglamorous, essential work of keeping the sector alive, funded, and growing.

Why State-Level Advocacy Is the Whole Game

Ask most school choice supporters what they know about their state's charter association, and you'll get a blank stare. But ask a charter school principal about the last time a state budget threatened to cut per-pupil funding, or a board chair about the year they almost lost their lease because a hostile district blocked their facility access—and suddenly the value of organized advocacy becomes very concrete.

Charter schools, unlike traditional public schools, have no automatic political constituency. They don't have teachers' unions running phone banks, and they don't have district central offices with decades of capital relationships at the statehouse. What they have—when they're lucky—is a state association that shows up to legislative hearings, tracks hostile bill language, and builds the relationships that translate into policy wins over time.

The 2025 legislative cycle made this visible on a national scale. Across the country, charter school advocates secured major wins in state legislatures—advancing funding equity, facilities access, and growth opportunities. The list is striking: Colorado raised the cap on its Moral Obligation Program to $1 billion; Illinois increased its charter facilities fund by $20 million; Indiana secured local property tax sharing for charters; Missouri appropriated $10 million to launch a new charter school facilities fund; and Tennessee secured $25 million in charter facilities support. North Dakota, remarkably, became the 47th state to allow charter schools at all.

None of that happened automatically. It happened because organized advocates—mostly state associations—put in years of relationship-building, lobbying, and coalition work to make it possible.

What a Strong State Association Actually Does

What does a state charter school association do? It's a fair question, and the answer is more practical than most people expect.

A well-functioning state charter association typically serves its member schools on three fronts:

  • Advocacy and lobbying — Monitoring legislation, maintaining a legislative agenda, building relationships with lawmakers, and mobilizing the charter community when a key vote is imminent.
  • Education and professional development — Convening school leaders, board members, and teachers to share what works; offering training on governance, compliance, and instructional models.
  • Member support and community — Providing access to job boards, legal and operational resources, peer networks, and a unified voice that any individual school would struggle to sustain alone.

The North Carolina Assocation for Public Charter Schools is a textbook example of this model in action. The organization describes its mission as built on three pillars—support, education, and advocacy—for charter schools across the state, with membership open to charter schools, management organizations, and businesses that support the sector. Its annual conference, held at the Raleigh Convention Center, is described as the largest gathering of charter school leaders, board members, teachers, and advocates in the state. That kind of convening power matters: it's where relationships get built and where a distributed network of schools starts to feel and function like a movement.

The Infrastructure Problem No One Talks About

Here's the uncomfortable reality beneath North Carolina's record enrollment numbers: the infrastructure supporting that growth is stretched thin.

The state's Office of Charter Schools grew from monitoring 96 schools in 2009 to 213 this year while its staff stayed largely flat. Seven consultants now handle applications, monitoring, the ready-to-open process, and closures. When the director of that office told the state's Charter Schools Review Board that "with greater numbers of staff, we could do more things that we would like to do," she was describing a sector that has outrun its oversight capacity.

That gap is exactly why the work of outside organizations—state associations, research partners, advocacy groups—becomes load-bearing infrastructure, not a nice-to-have. Charter schooling is now the fastest-growing segment of K-12 enrollment in North Carolina, posting a 6.1% annual enrollment increase. Fast growth without organized support is how you get schools that open unprepared, waitlists that never convert to seats, and facilities crises that kill promising programs before they prove themselves.

North Carolina ranks fourth in the nation for charter enrollment growth over the past six years, behind only Texas, Florida, and California. Sustaining that trajectory—and turning it into lasting quality, not just headcount—will require exactly the kind of sustained advocacy and support work that state associations exist to provide.

The Part Families Should Understand

Parents choosing a charter school for their child are making a deeply personal decision. They're picking a learning environment, a community, a values fit. They're probably not thinking about who lobbied against the bill that would have capped the school's enrollment, or which organization ran the training that helped their principal avoid a governance pitfall that sinks other charter boards.

But those things matter, and they connect directly to whether the school their child attends is stable, funded, and growing in the right direction.

The most effective charter school movements in the country—the ones producing real gains for kids who need better options—aren't just collections of good individual schools. They're ecosystems: schools doing great instructional work, backed by associations doing great policy and support work, in states where lawmakers have been persuaded that both are worth protecting.

North Carolina's record charter enrollment is a reason to celebrate. It's also a reminder that the next 59,000 students on a waitlist somewhere are counting on the whole ecosystem to hold.
