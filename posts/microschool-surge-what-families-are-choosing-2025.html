The Microschool Surge: What Families Are Choosing in 2025

# The Microschool Surge: What Families Are Choosing in 2025

Something is happening in American education that policymakers have barely begun to regulate. Microschools — intentionally small, often informal learning communities built around individual kids rather than institutional systems — are growing fast, and the families fueling that growth are not a fringe group. They are ordinary parents who looked at their options and decided that smaller was better.

What Is a Microschool, Exactly?

A microschool is not just a small private school with a quirky name. Microschools typically are small, have multi-age classrooms, and focus on self-paced learning — but they vary tremendously in setting, size, and focus. Most microschools (55%) operate under homeschool laws, while others function as private schools (37%), charters (6%), or fall into unique state categories (3%). They can meet in homes, churches, storefronts, or outdoor spaces, and their legal structure often depends entirely on the state where they operate.

Size is the most defining characteristic. Often described as "homeschool hybrids" or "small-scale learning communities," microschools often serve around 16 students or fewer. That intimacy is precisely the point. When a class has 10 to 15 students instead of 25 or 30, the whole dynamic changes — educators can build real relationships with each child, a student who needs encouragement is more likely to get it, and a student who is ready to move ahead does not have to wait for the rest of the class to catch up.

How Big Is the Microschool Movement in 2025?

As of 2025, about 2% of K-12 students in the U.S. — roughly 750,000 children — are enrolled in microschools, and some estimates suggest the real number may approach 2 million. Both figures reflect how difficult microschools are to count: many operate quietly under homeschool exemptions with no formal reporting requirement. The National Microschooling Center's 2025 sector analysis, which surveyed more than 800 currently operating or soon-to-open microschools representing all 50 states, found that 81% of microschools tracking academics report one to two years of academic growth within a single school year.

Charter schools, private schools, microschools, and homeschool pods are all drawing families in with flexibility, personalization, and often smaller environments. Homeschooling doubled in the U.S. between 2020 and 2023, and charter schools have grown by more than 1.6 million students since 2000 — microschools are surging alongside both of those trends, not instead of them.

The pandemic accelerated everything. In the five years since remote schooling amid pandemic-era lockdowns, microschools and homeschooling have emerged as increasingly popular alternatives to traditional public and private models. But unlike improvised pandemic pods, today's microschools are deliberate, mission-driven choices built to last.

Why Are Parents Choosing Microschools?

What drives families to microschools in 2025? The short answer, backed by survey data: they want their child seen as an individual. Sixty percent of parents searched for a new school for at least one child last year — with even higher rates among younger parents — signaling a broad shift away from default enrollment in assigned neighborhood public schools.

Based on an EdChoice survey conducted in partnership with KaiPod Learning, three in five parents are motivated by the more flexible schedule microschools offer, and nearly half cited small class sizes as the reason they switched. That preference translates directly into satisfaction: more than three in four microschooling parents (76%) report being "very satisfied" with the experience, while another 17% are "somewhat satisfied" — leaving only 5% expressing any dissatisfaction at all. A 2024 survey found that 37% of parents are more interested in microschools or hybrid options than they were before the pandemic, a number that continues to climb.

The top reasons families cite for choosing microschools include:

  • Personalized pacing — children advance when they are ready, not when a bell rings
  • Smaller class sizes — in a 2025 EdChoice and KaiPod Learning survey, 49% of microschool families cited this as a key reason they chose microschooling
  • Flexible scheduling — year-round, part-time, or project-based calendars that traditional schools rarely offer
  • Community and belonging — a sense of genuine connection that many large, institutional schools struggle to provide
  • Support for learning differences — many microschools specifically serve students with ADHD, autism, or trauma backgrounds who thrive in smaller, personalized environments

More than four out of ten microschool students (43%) previously attended traditional district schools — meaning most families choosing microschools are not long-time ideological homeschoolers. They are families who tried the conventional path and concluded it was not working well enough for their child.

Microschools and Charter Schools: Different Tools, Same Core Values

It is easy to frame microschools and charter schools as competitors for the same pool of dissatisfied families. In practice, they often reflect the same underlying values — personalization, community, and accountability to families rather than to bureaucracies — expressed at very different scales and through very different structures.

Microschools take different organizational forms, including learning centers that follow a state's homeschooling rules, small private schools that charge tuition, or even individual charter schools operating with a microschool philosophy. Their learning calendars vary from year-round to part-time to following a typical academic year. Charter schools, by contrast, offer the accountability structures, certified teachers, state assessments, and free tuition that many families still want and need. In states where education savings accounts have expanded, some charter schools are experimenting with microschool-style models within their walls — small cohorts, project-based learning, and flexible scheduling that would have been unthinkable in a traditional district school a decade ago.

Freedom Prep, a Utah charter school known formally as Freedom Preparatory Academy, represents the kind of institution that sits at this intersection: a publicly accountable school with a mission centered on meeting individual student needs — the same core promise driving families toward microschools, delivered through the transparency and accessibility of a free public charter education.

What the Microschool Surge Means for K-12 Policy

Education innovators are particularly susceptible to the pitfall of repeating history. With so many new people entering the school start-up space in the wake of massive expansions of school choice programs, education entrepreneurs risk stumbling into the same challenges that have ensnared others before them. The charter school movement's three decades of hard-won lessons about accountability, equity of access, and financial sustainability are directly relevant to where the microschool sector is headed.

States are responding to parent demand at different speeds. Utah has moved aggressively: for the 2025–2026 school year, Utah's Fits All Scholarship offers up to $8,000 per student that can be applied toward microschool tuition, curriculum, tutoring, and more — making the once-exclusive microschool model accessible to families across income levels. Utah has also eased building regulations and allowed microschools to operate in all zones, removing structural barriers that kept the model out of reach for many communities.

The critical unresolved question remains equity. Most microschools tend to cater to families with average or above-average incomes, and quality options remain unevenly distributed across geography, income, and race. That is a challenge the charter sector knows intimately, and one the microschool movement will need to confront honestly as it matures and seeks broader public support.

The Bottom Line for Families Considering Their Options

The microschool movement is not a fad, and it is not a threat to every other education model in its path. It is a signal — a clear and growing one — that a meaningful share of American families believe the structure of mass schooling was not designed with their specific child in mind. True school choice means empowering families to choose, or even create, the learning environment that works best for them — and microschooling embodies that principle by putting power in the hands of parents, educators, and communities closest to the student.

Whether a family ultimately lands on a microschool, a charter school, or a thoughtful combination of both, the underlying demand is the same: a school that knows their kid by name, teaches to their strengths, and does not make them wait for everyone else to catch up. That demand is reshaping K-12 education from the bottom up, one small classroom at a time.
