What Parents Really Want From Schools in 2025

# What Parents Really Want From Schools in 2025 — And Why the Answers Matter

Something significant has shifted in how American families think about K–12 education. School choice — once considered a niche concern for a specific slice of the population — has quietly become a mainstream expectation. The data from the past two years makes that transformation impossible to ignore.

According to a nationally representative survey of 3,960 U.S. parents conducted in December 2025 by the National School Choice Awareness Foundation, 75% of U.S. parents — roughly 46 million families — considered, searched for, or enrolled at least one child in a new or different school during 2025. That is the highest level recorded in five years of tracking, and it represents a near-unbroken upward trend: 52% in 2022, 54% in 2023, 60% in 2024, and now 75% in 2025.

The question worth asking isn't just how many parents are searching. It's what they're actually looking for — and whether schools are built to deliver it.

Safety and Belonging Come Before Academics

The top priorities parents name when enrolling a child in a new school might surprise people who assume academics always lead. In the 2025–2026 survey data, parents who successfully enrolled a child ranked their criteria this way:

  • 61% cited safety as their top priority
  • 47% wanted a positive social environment for their child
  • 39% named educational themes or specialized approaches

A separate National Alliance for Public Charter Schools survey reinforced this pattern: 77% of parents called safety "absolutely essential" to their child's education — more than any other factor. The quality of instruction came second, cited as absolutely essential by 58%.

This isn't parents saying academics don't matter. It's parents saying that a child cannot learn in a school where they don't feel safe, seen, or like they belong. The building blocks of student achievement — engagement, attendance, effort, persistence — are all downstream of whether a child feels secure and connected to their school community.

Why Parents Are Leaving Their Assigned Schools

Not everyone searching for a new school is fleeing a crisis. The most common reason parents gave for switching was simply dissatisfaction or a desire for a better fit (32%). Routine transitions — a child moving to a new school level, starting school for the first time, or a family relocation — accounted for most of the rest.

What's telling is who is most likely to search. Black parents (68%) and Hispanic parents (63%) searched for new schools at higher rates than white parents (58%). Younger parents aged 18–29 were more likely to search (71%) than any other age group. Military families were the most active of all, with 84% considering new schools in the 2025 cycle — and 91% expecting to search again in 2026.

These are not the demographics of families who have every advantage and are shopping for extras. They are often the families for whom the assigned neighborhood school has historically been the least responsive to their children's actual needs. The surge in school searching among these groups is a signal that more families have come to understand they have options — and are willing to use them.

What Happens When Parents Actually Switch

Skeptics sometimes argue that school switching is disruptive to children and rarely pays off. The parent survey data pushes back on that directly.

Among parents whose children switched school types, 89% reported a positive change as a result. The most common positive outcome was simply that their child was happier (57%). That's not a trivial result. Student happiness — or more precisely, the sense of belonging and engagement that underlies it — is one of the strongest predictors of academic persistence, attendance, and long-run educational attainment that researchers have documented.

Twenty-eight percent of parents who searched ultimately followed through with enrollment. Of those, 60% chose public-sector schools (including charter schools), 10% chose full-time online schools, and 30% chose private schools, homeschooling, or microschools. Contrary to the narrative that school choice is primarily a private school subsidy, public options — including public charter schools — remain the most common destination for families who make a change.

The Barriers That Stop Parents From Following Through

Here's where the picture becomes more complicated. In 2025, roughly 20 million parents searched for a new school but did not enroll their child. The barriers they cited were:

  • Concerns about disruption to their child's current experience (33%)
  • Uncertainty about making the wrong choice (26%)
  • Limited availability or waitlists (26%)
  • Transportation challenges (25%)

These are solvable problems — not philosophical objections to choice itself. Families aren't saying they don't want options. They're saying the process of finding and accessing those options is harder than it should be. Waitlists and transportation constraints in particular are policy and infrastructure problems, not reflections of low demand.

This is where statewide advocacy organizations play a critical role in making choice real rather than theoretical. The Public Charter School Alliance of South Carolina — the trusted voice for South Carolina's 111 public charter schools and the roughly 61,000 students they serve — works to ensure that the state's charter sector is strong, accessible, and well-resourced enough that when families go looking for a better fit, they can actually find one nearby.

What Schools That Win Parent Trust Actually Do

What do parents want from a school in 2025? Based on the survey data, the answer can be distilled into three things: a school where their child is safe, a school where their child belongs, and a school with a clear instructional identity — a specific approach, theme, or set of values that distinguishes it from the generic default.

Charter schools are structurally well-matched to that checklist. Because they are built around defined missions — whether that's project-based learning, classical curriculum, STEM focus, arts integration, or another specific approach — they naturally offer the kind of educational identity that 39% of searching parents said they were looking for. Because they tend to be smaller and more community-focused than large comprehensive district schools, they can more easily build the culture of safety and belonging that topped the priority list.

The 74% of parents who told the National Alliance for Public Charter Schools they would consider sending their child to a charter school if one were available in their area aren't expressing blind loyalty to a sector. They're expressing a preference for what charter schools, at their best, are designed to deliver: a school that was built with purpose, not just proximity.

The Implication for Families Still Searching

If you are among the families who searched but didn't enroll — or who haven't started searching yet but are feeling a pull to do so — the data suggests you're not alone and your instincts are worth following. The majority of parents who switched schools for their children are glad they did. The most common regret is waiting too long, not acting too soon.

Start with what matters most to your family: safety, community, a specialized program, or a teaching approach that fits your child's learning style. Then look at what's actually available — charter schools, magnet programs, online publics, private options — and visit in person when you can. Nearly half of enrolled parents (43%) said visiting schools in person was their most valuable step in the search process.

The landscape of K–12 education has more variety in it than the assigned-school model ever suggested. That variety exists because a generation of educators, advocates, and parents decided that more families deserved better options. The least parents can do now is use them.
