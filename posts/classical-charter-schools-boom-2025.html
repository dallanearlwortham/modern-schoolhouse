Why Classical Charter Schools Are Booming in 2025

Classical Education Was Supposed to Be a Niche. Parents Didn't Get the Memo.

A few years ago, "classical education" conjured images of private academies with Latin mottos and steep tuition bills — the kind of schooling reserved for families who could afford it. That picture is now obsolete. Across the country, tuition-free public charter schools are adopting classical and liberal arts curricula at a striking pace, and families are flooding waitlists to get in.

Classical education has become one of the brightest spots in the K–12 landscape. Once a niche movement, it now encompasses more than 1,500 public, private, and charter schools serving nearly 700,000 students. By 2035, projections suggest that number could reach 2,600, enrolling well over a million children. That isn't a niche. That's a movement — and it raises a serious question for any family currently evaluating school options: what's actually driving it, and does the model deliver?

What Is Classical Education, Really?

Classical education is heavily oriented toward the liberal arts, guided by the Western canon, and grounded in traditions of academic excellence stretching back to ancient Greece and Rome. In practice, students spend serious time on literature, history, logic, rhetoric, science, and often Latin or a second language. What they spend less time on is the kind of test-prep-driven, skills-fragmented instruction that dominates many conventional public schools.

Families are drawn to these schools for their rigorous liberal arts curriculum, their commitment to moral and intellectual formation, and their deliberate rejection of faddish pedagogies in favor of enduring works and ideas. That's not marketing language — it reflects a genuine philosophical difference in how students are taught to read, reason, and write.

The post-pandemic period dramatically accelerated family interest. The prolonged school closures triggered by COVID-19 gave parents a front-row seat in their children's schooling, and many were troubled by what they saw — both the volume of lost learning and the nature of the content being taught. As their concerns were dismissed or ignored, a large number of families began actively searching for something different. Classical schools were a primary beneficiary of that search. According to a market analysis by Arcadia Education, classical school enrollment for the 2023–2024 school year is estimated at over 677,500 students across 1,551 schools. Between 2019 and 2023, 264 new classical schools were started, at an average growth rate of 4.8% per year.

What Does the Research Say About Student Outcomes?

The short answer to the question "do classical charter schools actually work?" is: the best ones produce results that are hard to argue with.

In 2025, the four Bronx Classical Charter Schools averaged a proficiency rate of 96 percent for English and 98 percent for mathematics. By comparison, the average proficiency rate in the surrounding school districts where those schools are located was about 41 percent for English and 42 percent for math. That is not a marginal edge — it is a chasm. Critics who assume charter schools achieve strong outcomes by pushing out low-performing students find little support here: the network has never expelled a student, and enrollment numbers remain steady, with departing students' spots quickly filled by newcomers.

More broadly, classical-model charter schools in cities like New York, Washington D.C., and Nashville — each serving a majority-minority student body — have produced state test scores that match or exceed their respective public school district averages. Classical education is not proving itself only for affluent or already high-achieving students. It is working across economic and geographic lines, in communities that have historically been underserved by traditional district schools.

A Tuition-Free Classical Charter in Action

One concrete illustration of what the model looks like on the ground: The Vanguard School, a tuition-free K–12 charter school in Colorado Springs serving students from across the Pikes Peak Region. Now celebrating 30 years of excellence, the school uses a classical, liberal arts curriculum built on the Hillsdale Academy Model — a content-rich approach emphasizing literacy, history, the natural sciences, and the fine arts — with the explicit goal of giving every student the solid academic grounding required for success as an adult. The school runs a full continuum from kindergarten through 12th grade, including dedicated college counseling and a Global Studies program, all at no cost to families. It is precisely the kind of rigorous, purposeful schooling that, a generation ago, most families could only access by writing a private school tuition check.

Why Parents Are Actively Searching — and What to Watch For

A January 2024 parent survey by the National School Choice Awareness Foundation found that 72% of parents considered new schools for their children in the prior year — a 35% increase over 2022. Furthermore, 63% searched for a new school, and 44% actually chose a new school for their child. Classical charters have captured a meaningful share of that demand.

But not every school that uses the word "classical" on its website delivers the same experience. Families doing their homework should ask pointed questions before enrolling:

  • What does the daily curriculum actually look like? A genuine classical program features close reading of primary texts, Socratic discussion, and structured writing — not loosely themed projects or passive video consumption.
  • How is early literacy taught? Look for explicit, systematic phonics instruction paired with a content-rich read-aloud program in the elementary grades.
  • Is the school publicly authorized and tuition-free? Classical charter schools are publicly funded and admission is typically by lottery — no tuition, no selective entrance exam.
  • What do the state assessment results show? Ask for actual proficiency data, not just anecdotes, and compare it to the district average.
  • How does the school support and develop its teachers? Schools serious about classical pedagogy invest in ongoing teacher formation, not just initial hiring.

The One Real Obstacle to Growth: Teachers

Despite the sector's momentum, classical charters face a persistent and well-documented bottleneck: finding enough qualified teachers. Unlike district schools, classical institutions cannot simply recruit from the standard pipeline of education school graduates. Many school leaders actively prefer teachers with deep subject-matter expertise — people steeped in literature, history, philosophy, and science — over those whose training might conflict with classical pedagogy or school culture.

Some networks are engineering their own solutions. Classical Charter Schools in the Bronx runs a program called ClassiCorps — a three-year fellowship in which fellows teach during the day and earn a master of arts in teaching at night. Approximately 90 percent of their teachers come through this program. Other networks are partnering with universities offering master's programs in classical teaching, or building residency-style apprenticeships modeled loosely on programs like Teach For America.

These are creative workarounds, but they underscore how much infrastructure the classical movement still needs to build before supply can fully meet the demand families are generating. Without a robust teacher pipeline, the risk is that growth either stalls or — worse — schools begin compromising on the very characteristics that make families choose them in the first place.

The Bigger Picture

The surge in classical charter enrollment is ultimately a story about what happens when parents get real choices. When families can access a rigorous, content-rich education for their children — at no cost, in a publicly accountable school — a significant and growing number of them choose it. The academic results at well-run classical charters are difficult to dismiss, family demand shows no signs of cooling, and the tuition-free charter model is making this kind of formation available to communities that never had it before.

The oldest approach to formal education may have a great deal to teach us about what students need right now.
