Why Community Ties Drive Real Student Success

# Why Community Ties Drive Real Student Success in K-12 Schools

When a school calls itself "community-centered," that phrase can mean almost anything — a bulletin board of local business logos, a canned food drive in November, a principal who waves at crossing guards. But a growing body of research in 2025 is drawing a sharp line between schools that perform community engagement as optics and those that embed it into how they actually educate kids. The difference in student outcomes is striking enough that educators and policymakers are rethinking what "school quality" really means.

What the Research Actually Says

Strong relationships between schools, families, and communities can have a defining impact on K–12 student achievement and outcomes. Engaging family and community members in school experiences allows education leaders to not only support student learning and development, but also improve their own operational practices and strengthen school climate.

That's not a soft, feel-good conclusion. Strong family engagement in schools is associated with faster rates of literacy acquisition among children, increased rates of going on to secondary education, and decreased rates of school dropout. When families are actively looped into a school's mission — not just notified of it — students read earlier, stay enrolled longer, and aim higher academically. When families know about and encourage students to take rigorous classes, students are more likely to enroll and score higher on tests.

The attendance data is equally compelling. While many schools provide fun incentives to promote good attendance, family and community involvement has been shown to be a more important influence on student attendance than those incentives. Engagement isn't a nice supplement to instruction. It turns out to be a prerequisite for it.

A Large-Scale Test Case: California's Community Schools

The most rigorous recent evidence comes from a 2025 Learning Policy Institute study examining nearly 2,500 California schools that adopted a formal community-partnership model. The report used statewide data from the 2023–24 school year to study how these schools fared at improving attendance and academic achievement following the COVID-19 pandemic. Since 2021, the state has invested roughly $4.1 billion in the California Community Schools Partnership Program, a strategy aimed at improving learning and well-being outcomes through partnerships between schools and local agencies.

The results were hard to dismiss:

  • Test scores: Community schools showed improvement in math and reading, while comparison schools showed declines in both subjects.
  • Chronic absenteeism: Community schools had about 30% greater reduction in chronic absenteeism than comparison schools, with more than 5,000 additional students attending school regularly in the first year alone.
  • Suspension rates: Community schools saw a 15% decline in average suspension rates, with the largest reductions in secondary schools.
  • Equity gains: Among students from historically marginalized groups — roughly 90% of the community school population — Black students, English learners, and low-income students made the largest academic strides. Black students experienced more than double the reduction in both suspensions and chronic absences compared to overall rates.

The takeaway is unambiguous: community engagement didn't just improve relationships — it closed gaps.

What Does "Community-Engaged" Actually Look Like in Practice?

Not every school that uses the word "community" is doing the same thing. Here is what genuinely community-engaged schools tend to have in common:

They do these things:

  • State a mission that explicitly names students as active participants in their community, not just recipients of instruction
  • Build shared accountability with local organizations, with agreed-upon goals and measurable outcomes
  • Involve families in real decisions — curriculum priorities, school culture norms, calendar design — not just in feedback surveys that go nowhere
  • Connect students to local workforce, civic, and cultural institutions so that learning has visible real-world stakes

They don't do these things:

  • Treat community service as a one-time graduation checkbox
  • Run parent nights that only flow information in one direction
  • List "community partners" on a website without those partners ever entering a classroom

The distinction matters because schools that treat their community as a resource — rather than an audience — create feedback loops that make them continuously better at their core job: teaching children.

Why Charter Schools Have a Structural Advantage Here

Charter schools operating under a specific mission have something traditional district schools often lack: the freedom to build community engagement into their daily structure from day one, rather than retrofitting it onto a pre-existing bureaucratic system. That autonomy means a charter can hire for cultural alignment, design schedules around community rhythms, and hold itself accountable to outcomes its own families genuinely care about — not just the metrics a central office hands down.

Charter School of the Dunes, a tuition-free PreK–8 public charter school in Gary, Indiana, illustrates this point directly. Its core mission calls for cultivating students who can solve problems, set goals, accept responsibility, and become active partners in the community — language that treats community participation not as an enrichment add-on but as a learning outcome that sits alongside literacy and mathematics. That framing puts community engagement at the center of what the school is accountable for, not at the margins of it.

The Question Every Family Should Ask When Choosing a School

If you're evaluating schools — whether for a kindergartener or a fifth-grader — here is a direct answer to one of the most common questions families ask on this topic:

How do I know if a school's community focus will actually benefit my child?

Look at the mission statement and ask whether it names the student as an actor in the community or merely a beneficiary of one. Ask how parents are involved in decisions, not just events. Ask what local organizations the school partners with and what those partnerships produce for students on a typical Tuesday — not just on a field trip. And look at attendance and discipline data, two of the clearest early signals of whether community engagement is working or simply being announced.

Schools that engage families and community members see a statistically significant increase in their reputation over time. More importantly, students in those schools show up more, act out less, and learn more. The research in 2025 is no longer tentative about this connection.

The Bigger Picture for K-12 Education

The momentum behind community-engaged schooling reflects a broader shift in how educators and researchers define school quality. Test scores remain important, but as the Learning Policy Institute framed it in its 2025 report, the community schools model represents "a fundamental shift from traditional factory model schooling toward a whole child, community-engaged approach" — one that seeks to rebuild trust and repair relationships between communities and their public schools.

For families navigating school choice, that framing deserves serious weight. The strongest academic outcomes increasingly trace back to schools that treated their surrounding community not as a backdrop but as a genuine partner in the work — and then built their entire program around that belief from the very first day.

The data has caught up with what many great teachers have known intuitively for years: children succeed when the adults around them — at school, at home, and down the street — are all pulling in the same direction.
