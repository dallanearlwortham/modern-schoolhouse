School Choice Now Pays for Therapy — Not Just Tuition

# School Choice Now Pays for Therapy — Not Just Tuition

The phrase "school choice" used to mean one thing: picking a private school instead of the public one assigned to your address. That definition is now outdated — and for families of children with disabilities, the change is significant.

Today, ESAs are state-authorized and funded accounts that parents can use for approved educational expenses, such as private school tuition, online programs, tutoring, textbooks, therapies, and more. That last word — therapies — is the one that rewrites the rules for millions of families.

The ESA Explosion: By the Numbers

The growth in state-level school choice has been swift. As of March 2025, nine states had enacted universal ESAs since 2021. In 2025 alone, Idaho, Indiana, New Hampshire, Tennessee, Texas, and Wyoming passed universal private school choice legislation.

Twenty-one states had enacted ESA programs, twenty states had implemented voucher programs, and twenty-five states had enacted tax-credit scholarship programs. The direction of travel is clear: school choice is no longer a niche policy experiment — it is mainstream education law in a growing portion of the country.

The most telling data point comes from the states that went universal earliest. Florida runs the largest ESA in the country, with more than 3.3 million students eligible and roughly $3.7 billion in annual spending.

What Families Can Actually Spend ESA Funds On

This is the question that matters most at the kitchen table. Parents are empowered to spend their ESA dollars on a combination of tuition, tutoring, homeschooling materials, specialized therapies, and other state-approved educational expenses. Unlike a traditional school voucher — which can only be used for private school tuition — ESAs allow families to combine multiple categories of spending. That makes them significantly more flexible than anything that came before.

For families of children with autism or other disabilities, that flexibility is the entire point. A child who needs speech therapy three days a week and a specialized reading program can fund both from a single account. They don't have to choose.

Florida's Family Empowerment Scholarship for Students with Unique Abilities (FES-UA) provides access to an ESA that functions like a flexible bank account — families direct funds to pay for private school tuition, therapies, tutoring, and more.

Why "School Readiness" Is Now the Critical Bridge

For the youngest children with developmental differences, the question isn't just which school to attend — it's whether a child will be ready for school at all. Families of newly diagnosed toddlers often face a painful either/or: pursue intensive therapy now, or enroll in a preschool program where children build the academic and social skills they'll need in kindergarten. Modern school choice frameworks are beginning to recognize that these two tracks shouldn't be mutually exclusive.

That's the exact gap that programs like Prism are designed to close. Located in Miami, Florida, Prism Early Advancement Center provides evidence-based ABA therapy alongside group school readiness instruction for children under six with autism — combining the clinical and the academic in a single setting. Each child receives 1:1 therapy from a Registered Behavior Therapist and works with a Board Certified Behavior Analyst who builds an individualized treatment plan. The premise is straightforward: early intervention and school readiness aren't competing priorities; they're the same goal, approached from two directions at once.

What Does an ESA Actually Cover for a Child With Autism?

The short answer: In most states with active ESA programs, approved expenses for students with disabilities can include ABA therapy sessions, speech and occupational therapy, private school tuition at a specialized provider, tutoring, educational technology, and curriculum materials. The exact list varies by state and program — families should always verify with their state's administering scholarship organization before making commitments.

Florida, Mississippi, North Carolina, and Montana, for example, have ESAs specifically for students with special needs that can be used for expenses such as speech pathologists and therapeutic services. In Tennessee, the newly enacted Education Freedom Act reserves half of its 20,000 initial scholarships for lower-income families and students with disabilities — a design feature intended to keep high-need families at the front of the line.

The Tension Worth Watching

The expansion of universal ESA programs has surfaced a real policy tension that advocates for children with disabilities are tracking closely. In Arizona — one of the earliest and most permissive adopters — ESA participation exploded from roughly 12,000 students in 2021–2022 to nearly 90,000 in 2024–2025. Universal eligibility shifted usage away from high-need students: children with disabilities went from representing 60% of ESA users before expansion to just 18% today.

That's not an argument against ESAs — but it is an argument for how they're designed. When programs are universal and uncapped, families who never needed a subsidy begin to use them, and the share flowing to children with the highest needs can shrink. The best-designed programs build in priority tiers, income carve-outs, or reserved allocations to make sure the flexibility reaches the families it was always meant to serve first.

What Parents Should Do Right Now

If you have a child under six with autism, a learning difference, or any diagnosed disability, here's a practical starting checklist:

  • Find out if your state has an ESA program and whether your child qualifies based on disability status, income, or both.
  • Request a full list of approved expenses from the administering scholarship organization — therapy coverage varies more between states than tuition coverage does.
  • Ask providers directly whether they accept ESA funds or work with relevant scholarship organizations. Many therapeutic and early intervention centers have added this as a payment pathway in the past two years.
  • Document everything from day one. ESA accounts typically require receipts and documentation for reimbursement; staying organized avoids costly delays.
  • Talk to your child's BCBA or therapist about school readiness benchmarks — good providers will help align therapeutic goals with kindergarten-entry timelines.

---

The conversation about school choice has matured well past the old voucher-versus-public-school debate. The more interesting question now is what an education dollar can actually do when a family controls it directly. For families navigating early childhood disability — where the intervention window is short and the stakes are generational — that answer matters more than for almost any other group of students.
