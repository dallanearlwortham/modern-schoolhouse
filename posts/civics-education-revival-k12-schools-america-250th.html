Why Civics Is Making a Comeback in K-12 Schools

# Why Civics Is Making a Comeback in K-12 Schools

America just turned 250. And across the country, that milestone is doing something educators have struggled to accomplish for decades: putting civics back at the center of K–12 learning.

It is not a small shift. Since 2021, a total of 34 states have passed legislative bills to strengthen their civics education policies, and Congress has quadrupled funding for K–12 civics. After years of being squeezed out by standardized testing pressure in reading and math, history and citizenship are finally getting their moment — and the timing could not be more urgent.

The Civics Gap Is Real — and Alarming

Before celebrating the revival, it is worth understanding what is being revived from.

In 2024, the U.S. Chamber of Commerce Foundation surveyed 2,000 registered voters to test basic civic knowledge — and over 70% failed. Remarkably, most of those respondents said they had studied civics in high school. The knowledge simply did not stick.

Today's students are not faring much better. The National Assessment of Educational Progress civics exam found that nearly one-third of eighth graders are performing below the NAEP "basic" level in civics — meaning many cannot explain the structure and functions of government. Only 49% of eighth-graders reported taking a class primarily focused on civics or the U.S. government, according to the U.S. Department of Education in 2022. Confidence is declining too: a 2022 survey accompanying the NAEP exam showed that more students reported low confidence in their civics knowledge than in 2018.

How did we get here? Two trends drove the decline. First, the emphasis placed on core subjects such as math, reading, and science — backed by mandated annual testing — caused many schools to cut or reduce civics and social studies to make room. Second, the growing debate about what constitutes "quality" civics education led many schools to reduce it to a bare minimum rather than risk controversy. The result, as Harvard's Danielle Allen has described it, is that "polarization is what has kept students from having rich civics educational experiences."

America's 250th: A Turning Point for Civic Learning

What is driving the current civics education revival in K-12 schools? The simplest answer: a birthday. America's semiquincentennial — the 250th anniversary of the nation's founding, marked on July 4, 2026 — has created a rare, bipartisan window of energy and investment around civic education at every level.

Growing numbers of schools that had relegated civics classes to after-school activities are bringing back civics as a graded course. National and grassroots organizations such as the Center for Civic Education, iCivics, Civics Now, and the Bill of Rights Institute are working with state boards of education to create new curricula and opportunities for students to demonstrate their knowledge in competitions and public forums.

The results are beginning to show. More than 70% of Americans can now name the three branches of government, according to a September 2025 report from the Annenberg Public Policy Center of the University of Pennsylvania — up from just 26% a decade before. That is a dramatic improvement, even if the baseline was painfully low.

Twelve states — representing 700,000 students — have adopted new middle school and high school course requirements. The National Assessment of Educational Progress is also expanding and revising its civics assessment tests and will provide state-by-state comparisons for eighth graders for the first time, creating new accountability for how well schools are actually delivering on civic learning.

As iCivics' policy leader Shawn Healy has said, "There's increasingly a bipartisan consensus that schools have this historic mission to develop young people for their engagement in our constitutional democracy, and we are largely failing at that mission to our detriment. So there's just renewed energy here."

What Good Civics Education Actually Looks Like

Not all civics instruction is equal. Research is beginning to clarify what actually moves the needle for students.

According to a 2024 analysis from the Educating for American Democracy Research and Evaluation Task Force, released during Civic Learning Week, students learn more when they are in classrooms where civic and political issues are discussed freely and openly — and high-quality assessments and accountability structures lead to measurable increases in civic knowledge. When students engage in asking and answering questions by analyzing information, they experience greater engagement and deeper understanding than if they just use a textbook. Social and emotional learning and civic learning are also often mutually reinforcing: students' social, cognitive, and emotional skills help them critically and collaboratively engage with civic issues in ways that pure memorization never could.

One finding cuts across all of this: the earlier civic learning starts, the better. Elementary-age students who build a strong foundation in history, geography, and how government works are far better positioned to engage meaningfully in middle and high school civics courses. That is one reason the current revival is not stopping at eighth grade — it is reaching all the way down into elementary classrooms. Research from CIRCLE at Tufts University also shows that students who have positive civic experiences in high school, where they feel their voice matters, are significantly more likely to vote and remain civically engaged as adults.

Why This Matters for Families Choosing a School

For families navigating school choice, the civics revival is a genuinely useful lens for evaluating options. A school's commitment to history and citizenship is not just about test scores — it shapes how students understand themselves as participants in a community and a democracy.

This is exactly the kind of explicit mission that draws families to certain charter schools. Jefferson Academy, a tuition-free public charter elementary school in Kaysville, Utah serving grades K–6, is built around a history-focused curriculum that places American citizenship and foundational knowledge of world and American history at the core of every grade level — framing its entire mission around developing "informed, compassionate, global citizens." That kind of institutional clarity about why history matters, embedded from kindergarten onward, is precisely what education researchers say makes civic learning stick over time rather than evaporate after a unit test.

There is another dimension worth noting. Research from More in Common found that few Americans have civic role models — but those who do most often identify parents or family members. That finding points directly to why schools should partner actively with families on civic formation, rather than treating citizenship education as something that happens only inside classrooms during a single semester course.

Questions to Ask When Evaluating Any School

If civic preparedness matters to your family, here are the right questions to raise during school tours or enrollment conversations:

  • Does history and social studies get dedicated instructional time every week, K–12? Or does it disappear when test season arrives?
  • Are students learning to discuss civic and political issues openly, in a structured, evidence-based way — or is controversy avoided entirely?
  • Does the school's stated mission explicitly mention citizenship or civic formation, not just academic achievement metrics?
  • Are families treated as partners in civic learning, or merely notified of school events?
  • Does the curriculum build sequentially year over year, so civic knowledge compounds rather than resets each September?

Schools that can answer yes to most of these — whether traditional district schools or tuition-free charter schools — are doing more than preparing students for standardized tests. They are preparing them for the responsibilities of self-governance.

The Bigger Picture

The 250th anniversary of America's founding need not be only a great day of fireworks and reflection on our beginnings. It can also mark the start of a prolonged period of national rediscovery — of our shared history, our founding ideals, and the rights and responsibilities inherent in American citizenship.

For K–12 schools, that means treating civics not as an add-on subject squeezed into a single semester, but as a throughline — starting in kindergarten, building year by year, and connecting history to the present in ways students can feel. As Thomas Jefferson wrote, "The people are the safe depositories of their own liberty" — but only if they are enlightened. That was true in 1776. It is still true today.

America's 250th will eventually fade from the headlines. The schools that take this moment seriously, building civic knowledge from the earliest grades with intention and consistency, are planting seeds that will outlast the celebration by generations.
