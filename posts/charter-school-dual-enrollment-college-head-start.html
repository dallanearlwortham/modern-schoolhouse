How Charter Schools Are Turning High School Into a College Head Start

# How Charter Schools Are Turning High School Into a College Head Start

For most of the twentieth century, high school and college were two distinct phases separated by a clean gap. Students finished one, then started the other. That boundary is dissolving — and charter schools are among the most aggressive institutions pushing it aside.

Dual enrollment, which allows high school students to earn college credits while still completing their secondary education, has grown from a quiet workaround into a mainstream pathway. Each year, nearly 2.5 million high school students take dual enrollment college courses, now offered at 90% of public high schools across the country. But access is not evenly distributed — and that's where charter schools, including fully online ones, are playing an increasingly important role.

What Dual Enrollment Actually Means in 2025

Dual enrollment is straightforward in concept: a high school student enrolls in a college course, earns credit toward their diploma, and simultaneously banks transferable college credit. In most cases, the cost to the family is zero — the high school, district, or state covers tuition.

According to federal data, 2.8 million high school students were enrolled in at least one college class during the 2023–2024 academic year — making dual enrollment one of the fastest-growing trends in K–12 education. The appeal is direct: students reduce the time and cost of a college degree before they ever set foot on a campus full-time. Evidence from 2025 shows that participants benefit from improved college readiness, meaningful cost savings, and modest long-term earnings gains. But the benefits extend beyond dollars. Students who complete dual enrollment arrive at college having already demonstrated they can handle college-level work — and that changes everything about how they approach their freshman year.

The Research on Student Outcomes Is Striking

The most comprehensive look at dual enrollment outcomes came in October 2024, when the Community College Research Center (CCRC) at Columbia University released a national and state-by-state analysis tracking what happened to high school students who participated in early college programs. The findings were clear: dual enrollment students show higher rates of college-going and college completion than students who don't participate. Of the high schoolers tracked, more than 80% enrolled in college straight after high school, compared with roughly 70% of the general high school graduate population.

Nearly 40% of new undergraduates overall — and 60% of new community college students — were either current or former dual enrollment students. That statistic signals just how thoroughly early college credit has become embedded in how students navigate postsecondary education.

What does the research say about dual enrollment and long-term college success? Students who complete dual enrollment courses are more likely to enroll in college, more likely to persist past their first year, and tend to accumulate credits faster than peers who enter without any prior college experience. The mechanism isn't mysterious: dual enrollment builds both academic skill and self-confidence simultaneously, so students don't start from scratch when it matters most.

Why Charter Schools Are Particularly Well-Positioned

Traditional public schools offer dual enrollment too, but charter schools often have structural advantages that make it easier to build the program into the core student experience. Charter schools can design their entire academic model around early college preparation in ways that district schools — bound by more uniform scheduling, staffing, and curriculum mandates — often cannot.

Pennsylvania College of Technology entered into its first cyber charter dual enrollment partnership, offering classes to Insight PA Cyber Charter School students beginning in the 2025–2026 school year. That development is significant: even fully virtual charter schools are now creating credible, formal bridges between high school coursework and real college credit.

Online charter schools in particular face a perception challenge — families sometimes worry that a virtual setting means fewer academic opportunities. A robust dual enrollment program directly counters that narrative. A student at a virtual charter school who graduates with a semester's worth of transferable college credit already earned has received something most traditional high school graduates have not.

LANV — Leadership Academy of Nevada, a free online charter school serving grades 6–12 — has built dual enrollment into the fabric of what it offers students. On average, students who participate in LANV's dual enrollment program graduate with 20 college credits, the school reports. Combined with a four-year cohort graduation rate above 95% and ACT scores that consistently outpace the Nevada state average, the program illustrates what's possible when a charter school treats early college access as a core outcome rather than an optional add-on.

The Equity Angle Shouldn't Be Overlooked

One of the most compelling arguments for dual enrollment — especially in charter school settings — is what it does for students who would otherwise arrive at college carrying significant financial disadvantage.

The CCRC report noted that dual enrollment students' outcomes vary considerably across and within states, with important gaps in access and success for low-income, Black, and Hispanic students. That gap is a problem, but it also defines an opportunity: charter schools that serve historically underrepresented communities and actively integrate dual enrollment into their programming are giving those students something genuinely transformative — a head start that private school families have long purchased through AP programs, SAT prep, and college counselors.

Tuition-free dual enrollment effectively democratizes early college access. A student from a low-income household who earns 20 college credits before graduation isn't just saving money — they're compressing the timeline to a degree, which matters enormously when every additional semester in college carries a real financial cost.

What Families Should Ask Before Choosing a Dual Enrollment Program

Not all dual enrollment offerings are equal. Before assuming a charter school's program will deliver meaningful college credit, families should press on a few specifics:

  • Are the credits transferable? Credits earned through a community college partner typically transfer broadly; credits earned through a school-developed "dual enrollment" course may not travel as far.
  • Is there an academic readiness bar? Dual enrollment works best when students are genuinely prepared for college-level rigor. Programs that funnel every student in regardless of preparation may be setting some up for a damaging early academic experience.
  • How is the program integrated into the school's broader mission? A standalone dual enrollment option tacked onto a conventional high school curriculum is far less powerful than a school that builds college-level expectations into its culture from day one.
  • What is the average credit yield at graduation? Knowing how many credits participating students typically earn tells you whether the program is substantive or mostly nominal.
  • Are advising and support structures in place? Students navigating college coursework while still in high school benefit enormously from structured guidance — about credit applicability, course selection, and what specific credits mean for their intended college or career path.

High School Doesn't Have to End at Graduation Anymore

The most consequential shift dual enrollment represents isn't logistical — it's psychological. When a student earns a genuine college grade in a real college course while still in high school, they stop thinking of themselves as someone who will someday be ready for college. They start thinking of themselves as someone who already is.

That identity shift is difficult to manufacture through test prep or motivational programming. It happens through real academic challenge met and cleared — and the charter schools that have embedded dual enrollment into their model are creating it at scale, often for students who would never have had access to early college opportunity otherwise.

High school and college were always an artificial divide. The best charter schools in 2025 are simply making that obvious — and sending graduates into their freshman year with a semester's worth of credits, a proven track record, and a very different relationship to what they're capable of.
