Dual Credit in High School: The College Access Game-Changer

# Dual Credit in High School: The College-Access Strategy Every Family Should Know About

Millions of high schoolers are quietly earning college credits before they ever set foot on a college campus—and a growing body of research says it's one of the most powerful tools we have for helping students not just get to college, but stay there.

So why are so many students still missing out?

What Is Dual Enrollment—and Why Does It Matter?

Dual enrollment (also called dual credit or concurrent enrollment) lets high school students take college-level courses that count toward both a high school diploma and a college transcript. The courses can be offered on the high school campus, at a local community college, or online.

Figures released in August 2024 by the Department of Education show that, in the 2022–23 academic year, about 2.5 million high school students—roughly 16 percent—took at least one college course. By 2023–24, that number had climbed further still. The median state grew dual enrollment by 10%, and 26 states recorded double-digit gains, with particularly large increases in Delaware (+44%), Maryland (+32%), Nevada (+31%), Washington (+27%), and California.

The appeal is clear: students get a head start on college credits, often at little or no cost, while still inside the support structure of high school.

What the Research Actually Shows

Does dual enrollment improve college outcomes for high school students? The short answer is yes—with important caveats about who gets access and how programs are designed.

A landmark October 2024 analysis by the Community College Research Center (CCRC) at Teachers College, Columbia University, and the National Student Clearinghouse Research Center tracked every high school student who began taking dual enrollment classes in 2015. Of those roughly 400,000 students, more than 80 percent enrolled in college straight after high school—compared to 70 percent of high school graduates in the general population. Almost 30 percent of those dual enrollees—roughly 117,000 students—earned a bachelor's degree within four years.

Students gain an array of benefits from participating in dual enrollment: participation is associated with higher high school completion, college readiness, attendance, retention, and academic achievement. Dual enrollment students also enroll in college at higher rates, accumulate more credits, earn higher GPAs, and attain overall higher achievement in college.

A 2025 study reported that students who completed at least one dual-enrollment course were 1.5 times more likely to receive a scholarship in their first year of college compared to non-participants.

These are not trivial gains. For a student whose family has limited savings and no roadmap for navigating higher education, arriving at college with credits already banked—and a demonstrated record of college-level work—can be genuinely life-changing.

The Equity Problem Hiding Inside the Good News

Here's where it gets complicated. Dual enrollment's explosive growth has not been felt equally across student populations.

The CCRC's 2024 report found higher rates of college-going and completion for dual enrollment students overall—but also that outcomes vary greatly across and within states, with persistent gaps in access and success for low-income, Black, and Hispanic students.

White students are overrepresented in dual enrollment at 1.3 times their proportion of the student population, while Black students participate at just 0.6 times their representation, and Hispanic students at 0.7 times.

The reasons are structural. Research shows that dual enrollment options are less prevalent in schools primarily serving low-income students or students of color, and researchers point to a lack of funding as the main culprit. When states don't provide sufficient funding, high schools and colleges might not offer dual enrollment at all—or if they do, costs of tuition, textbooks, and other materials may fall entirely to students and families, putting participation out of reach.

The gap in outcomes is just as troubling as the gap in access. Of those who dual enrolled, only 28% of low-income students, 29% of Black students, and 25% of Hispanic students earned a bachelor's degree within four years, compared to 36% of all dual enrollment students. That disparity doesn't mean dual enrollment fails these students—it means the program design, advising infrastructure, and financial support around it still falls short for too many.

Why Charter High Schools Are Positioned to Close the Gap

This is where charter schools enter the picture—and where the conversation gets genuinely interesting.

Charter schools often serve the very students most underrepresented in dual enrollment: first-generation college-goers, students from low-income households, and students of color in urban communities. Because they operate with more scheduling and curriculum flexibility than traditional district schools, they can embed dual credit pathways directly into a student's four-year plan rather than treating them as an optional add-on reserved for high achievers.

Research demonstrates that when dual enrollment programs are designed well, they result in strong postsecondary outcomes—and that underrepresented students who do participate can see outsized gains relative to their peers. One October 2024 report found that students from underserved backgrounds who participated in dual enrollment actually improved more in outcomes than any other group by comparison.

The model works best when dual credit is woven into a school's core identity—not just listed on a flyer. When students receive proactive advising, have cost-free or remote access to courses, and see peers taking college classes as a normal part of junior and senior year, participation rates climb even among populations historically shut out of these opportunities.

That kind of intentional design is what separates schools that make dual credit a genuine college-access lever from those simply checking a compliance box. Sena HS, a charter high school serving grades 9–12 in Albuquerque, New Mexico, lists Dual Credit Opportunities directly among its core program offerings—embedding college-level coursework alongside an accelerated learning model and small class sizes as part of how it prepares every student for life after graduation, not just the ones who were already on a clear path forward.

What States Are Doing—and What Still Needs to Change

Policy momentum is real and accelerating. Several states passed standout dual enrollment legislation in 2025 with significant implications for access, quality, and equity—including Arkansas, which overhauled concurrent enrollment financing by establishing uniform statewide tuition guidelines and eliminating tuition and fee costs for participating students and families.

New York's State Education Department proposed bold statewide regulations in 2025 requiring school districts and charter schools to submit formal partnership agreements with colleges and to annually report participation and outcome data, which would be published publicly each year to ensure transparency. Connecticut passed legislation strengthening statewide dual enrollment quality and consistency, including mandating accreditation standards for concurrent enrollment courses on a defined timeline.

The goal across all of these efforts is the same: make dual enrollment a default opportunity, not a privilege reserved for students whose families already know how to navigate higher education systems.

What Families and Students Should Ask

If you're evaluating a high school—charter or otherwise—here are the right questions to ask about its dual credit program:

  • Is it free, or will my family pay tuition and fees? Cost is one of the single biggest participation barriers for low-income students.
  • Are the credits transferable? Credits that don't transfer to four-year universities can leave students feeling misled about the real value of the work they put in.
  • Who actually gets access? Is dual enrollment genuinely open to all students, or quietly steered toward those already performing at the top?
  • Is there dedicated advising support? Students who take college courses without guidance on how those credits fit into a larger academic plan often don't fully capitalize on the opportunity.
  • How are courses delivered? On-site instructors, community college campuses, and online formats each carry different implications for student experience and real-world success rates.

The Bottom Line

Dual enrollment is one of the most well-researched college-access tools in K–12 education right now—and the evidence is genuinely compelling. More than 80 percent of students who participate go on to enroll in college directly after high school. They earn better grades, accumulate more credits, and are more likely to finish a degree than peers who never took a college course in high school.

But that potential is still unevenly distributed. Without deliberate, equity-focused design—proactive outreach, eliminated costs, strong advising, and a school culture that treats college credit as every student's right rather than a reward for the already-advantaged—dual enrollment programs risk deepening the very gaps they promise to close.

Schools and policymakers that take that challenge seriously have a real opportunity to change college trajectories at scale. The research is there. The policy tools are taking shape. What's needed now is the institutional will to make sure the students who stand to gain the most are the ones actually sitting in those college classrooms.
