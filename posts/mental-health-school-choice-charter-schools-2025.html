The Mental Health Factor in School Choice (2025)

# The Mental Health Factor in School Choice: Why More Families Are Asking Different Questions in 2025

For a growing number of families weighing school options this year, the first question is no longer "What are the test scores?" It's "Will my kid be okay here?"

That shift isn't sentiment — it's data. More than half of public schools (52%) report they are not able to effectively provide mental health services to all students who need them, underscoring persistent gaps in staffing, funding, and provider capacity. The State of Student Mental Health Report 2025 from eLuma found that 57% of mental health providers reported a decline in student mental health in the past year — up from 46% in 2024 — while only 4% of respondents saw any improvement at all.

For parents navigating school choice, those numbers are impossible to ignore.

Why Student Mental Health Has Become a School-Choice Issue

The adolescent mental health crisis didn't arrive overnight. An estimated 31.9% of adolescents have an anxiety disorder. The percentage of 12–17 year-olds who experienced a major depressive episode in the past year rose to 18.1% — up from just 7.9% in 2006. Meanwhile, sleep deprivation compounds everything: up to 73% of teens aren't getting enough sleep, and chronic exhaustion undermines attention, mood regulation, and the ability to form the peer relationships that buffer against anxiety and isolation.

What's changed in 2025 is that parents are connecting these dots at enrollment time. Families aren't just asking about AP course offerings or graduation rates — they're asking about counselor-to-student ratios, social-emotional learning programs, and whether the school has a culture where a struggling ninth-grader will actually be noticed before things reach a crisis point.

The research backs up why that last part matters so much. School connectedness — the belief that adults and peers in a school genuinely care about a student as a person, not just as a test-taker — is consistently linked to better mental health outcomes, lower absenteeism, and reduced risk behaviors in adolescence and into adulthood. Yet in the 2024–2025 school year, only 18% of students utilized school-based mental health services, according to KFF — a figure that signals just how wide the gap between need and access remains.

What Does "Strong Mental Health Support" Actually Look Like at School?

If you're a parent evaluating options, the answer to this question matters more than any marketing brochure. A school with genuine mental health infrastructure typically shows several concrete signs:

  • A realistic counselor-to-student ratio. The American School Counselor Association recommends one counselor per 250 students. Most large public schools far exceed that. Smaller schools — including many charters — are better positioned to hit the target.
  • Proactive screening, not just crisis response. The earlier schools identify mental health needs, the better the outcomes. Look for schools that use structured check-ins or universal screeners rather than waiting for a student to reach a breaking point.
  • Trauma-informed staff throughout the building. At least 60% of students have experienced Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs), which shape how they respond to authority, deadlines, and peer conflict. Schools where every teacher has trauma-informed training handle these moments very differently than those where only the counselor does.
  • Meaningful family engagement. Parent engagement is a key factor in improving student mental health, yet many schools still treat family involvement as a peripheral add-on rather than an integrated part of student support.
  • Staff wellness as a priority, not an afterthought. This one is easy to overlook, but 48% of public school teachers report that their own mental health and wellness has had a negative impact on their teaching. Schools that invest in teacher wellbeing tend to sustain healthier student environments over time — it isn't a coincidence.

How Charter Schools Are Responding

The flexibility that defines charter schools — freedom from certain district mandates, autonomy in scheduling, staffing, and program design — is proving to be a meaningful structural advantage in the mental health space. Charter operators can partner with outside providers, build dedicated advisory or wellness periods into the school day, and cultivate a school culture from the ground up rather than retrofitting it onto an existing bureaucratic structure.

Some charters have moved well beyond hiring a single counselor. The Arts and Technology Academy of Pontiac in Michigan, for example, contracted with CNS Healthcare to establish a Community Behavioral Health Clinic right on campus — serving students, parents, and staff at no cost through a grant partnership. That model, embedding clinical-level care directly inside the school building, would be difficult to replicate within a large district without significant overhead. Meanwhile, State Street Academy in Bay City hired a dedicated wellness coach specifically to support teacher wellbeing, operating on the sound logic that a staff that is emotionally resourced delivers a dramatically different classroom experience than one running on empty.

Smaller school communities also structurally support school connectedness in ways that large comprehensive high schools simply cannot. When a freshman class numbers in the hundreds, it's genuinely possible for a student to slip through unnoticed for months. In a smaller community, every teacher tends to know every student — not as a data point, but as a person.

That community-scale advantage is exactly the kind of thing families increasingly weigh when choosing a school. It's part of what draws students to tuition-free charter high schools like Career Path High in Kaysville, Utah, a grades 9–12 charter where the smaller-school model creates the conditions for the adult-student relationships that research identifies as protective against the mental health struggles so many teenagers are navigating right now.

The Funding Threat Families Should Know About

There is a significant cloud over school-based mental health programs heading into the 2025–26 year. In April 2025, under the Trump administration, the Department of Education announced the cancellation of key federal grants previously used to fund school-based mental health services — funds authorized under the 2022 Bipartisan Safer Communities Act specifically to increase the number of mental health providers in schools and expand staff training.

For families choosing between schools, this creates a clarifying question worth asking directly: Has this school built mental health support into its core operating model — through community partnerships, staff training structures, advisory periods, and school culture — or did it bolt on a mental health program during flush grant years and depend on that funding to sustain it? Schools with internally woven, resilient support systems will weather this policy shift far better than those that didn't.

Six Questions to Ask on Your Next School Tour

Whether you're considering a charter, a traditional public school, or a magnet program, these questions cut through the marketing language and reveal what a school actually has in place:

  1. What is your current counselor-to-student ratio?
  2. Do you use any schoolwide mental health screening tools, and how often?
  3. How do teachers receive training in trauma-informed practices or social-emotional support?
  4. What happens when a student is clearly struggling but hasn't been formally referred — who notices, and what's the process?
  5. How does the school support staff mental health and guard against burnout?
  6. What community mental health partnerships or outside resources do you draw on beyond your in-house staff?

The schools that answer these questions fluently — with specifics, not brochures — are the ones that have genuinely made student wellbeing part of their institutional identity. The ones that fumble through them are telling you something important, too.

---

Academics matter. Graduation rates matter. Career preparation matters. But the question of whether a school will recognize a struggling teenager before things get critical — and whether the community around that student is equipped to respond — is increasingly the question that defines where families choose to enroll. In 2025, mental health support isn't a bonus feature. It's a core part of what a good school looks like.
