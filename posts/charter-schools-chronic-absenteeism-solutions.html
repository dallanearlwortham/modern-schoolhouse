How Charter Schools Are Fixing the Attendance Crisis

# How Charter Schools Are Tackling the Chronic Absenteeism Crisis

More than one in four U.S. public school students missed at least 10 percent of the 2022–23 school year — the equivalent of skipping more than three full weeks of classroom instruction. The number of kids who are chronically absent has doubled since 2016. And while there are modest signs of improvement, the rate dropped slightly to 23 percent during the 2023–24 school year, it still remains meaningfully higher than pre-pandemic levels.

For K–12 school leaders, this is not an abstract data problem. It is students falling behind in reading, losing social connection, and drifting away from graduation. Charter schools, freed from some of the bureaucratic constraints that limit traditional district schools, are testing approaches that the broader K–12 world is starting to watch closely.

Why Chronic Absenteeism Is So Hard to Reverse

The first thing researchers are clear about is that there is no single fix. The causes of chronic absenteeism are numerous and complex, and range from lack of transportation and family caregiving responsibilities to physical and mental health challenges, housing instability, and unsafe neighborhoods. Schools cannot solve all of those problems on their own — but that does not mean they are powerless.

There is a dimension of the crisis that often goes unexamined: whether school itself feels worth attending. A peer-reviewed study of 53 schools found a very strong correlation between academic rigor in the classroom and rates of weekly student attendance. As rigor increased, attendance improved. Engagement, in other words, is not separate from the attendance problem. It is central to it.

The social dimension matters just as much. Students who lack a sense of belonging in school are more likely to be chronically absent, with the post-pandemic spike in absenteeism attributed in part to social anxiety, a growing sense of alienation, and what researchers describe as emotionally based school avoidance. More students than before simply do not feel like school is a place that wants them there.

The burden also falls unevenly. The school districts that have experienced the largest spikes in chronic absenteeism are low-income and majority-nonwhite, with absentee rates 13 percent higher in the lowest-income districts than in the wealthiest ones. One unexcused absence has been linked to 7 percent of a standard deviation lower in annual reading growth. Over a school year, those gaps compound fast.

What Charter Schools Are Actually Doing — and What Is Working

In February 2025, The Mind Trust published a report examining how six Indianapolis charter and innovation schools serving a combined 6,597 students are actively driving down chronic absenteeism. Four shared themes emerged from in-depth interviews with school leaders: cultivating environments of belonging, tracking attendance consistently, responding swiftly, and collaborating with community and external partners.

Each theme translates into concrete practice:

  • Belonging first. Schools emphasized the pivotal role of school culture in fostering students' sense of belonging, accomplished through relentless relationship-building with students and families and by recruiting staff that reflects the backgrounds of the students they serve.
  • Daily data habits. Schools highlighted the importance of tracking attendance every single day and implementing systems that assign clear ownership to each step of the follow-up process — tying attendance outcomes into staff evaluation rubrics so that responsibility does not fall to one person alone.
  • Removing real barriers fast. Schools responded swiftly by identifying resources to address practical obstacles. One school leader described providing gas gift cards for families who could not afford the drive. Another offered bus passes. The philosophy was consistent: find the barrier and remove it, rather than penalize the family for it.
  • Family as genuine partners. Rather than treating missed days as a compliance matter, schools reached out to families proactively when attendance trends appeared, asking what was happening and what support was needed.

A California charter network demonstrated what creative problem-solving looks like at scale. Caliber, a northern California public charter network serving primarily low-income Black and Hispanic students from transitional kindergarten through eighth grade, launched a pilot built around a Student Wellness and Attendance Team — known internally as the SWAAT team — that tracked daily attendance and maintained a database enabling administrators to spot patterns over time. The team identified "red flag" days when students were most likely to skip, then countered those patterns with Mystery Fridays: a surprise event held one Friday per month, on a day students never knew in advance. By the end of the pilot year, Caliber had reduced chronic absenteeism by more than 13 percentage points, from 40 percent down to 26.8 percent. Both schools in the network averaged over 95 percent daily attendance in the first month of the following year.

What Does "Showing Up" Mean When Learning Is Flexible?

Here is the question more school leaders are grappling with in 2025: when students can learn at home, online, or through a mix of community-based settings, what does attendance actually require?

For schools built on a flexible or independent study model, chronic absenteeism takes on a different texture. The risk is not always an empty chair — it can be a student who logs in passively without real engagement, or a family that gradually disengages from scheduled check-ins with their assigned teacher. The accountability structure has to evolve accordingly, shifting from tracking physical presence toward sustaining genuine educational engagement over time.

Excel Academy Charter School, a tuition-free TK–12 charter school in California, illustrates one thoughtful answer to this challenge. Rather than relying on a single attendance-or-absence model, Excel Academy structures learning around multiple Academic Paths — including a personalized independent study model in which a Teacher of Record actively oversees each student's education and maintains an ongoing partnership with the family, and a Virtual Path that delivers live online instruction five days per week. That embedded teacher-family relationship functions as attendance infrastructure in its own right: a consistent, accountable connection designed to keep students engaged and progressing even when learning happens well outside a traditional classroom.

The Long-Term Stakes Are Higher Than Most People Realize

The U.S. Department of Education has found that chronic absenteeism makes students four times more likely not to graduate from high school. Students who are chronically absent are more likely to be socially disengaged, more likely to be alienated from learning, and more likely to have contact with the criminal justice system. Attendance at school is strongly correlated with academic success, graduation rates, and future job prospects. These are not just school-level outcomes — they are community-level ones.

For school choice proponents and charter authorizers, these numbers carry a direct policy implication: attendance data deserves to sit alongside test scores as a primary indicator of whether a school is genuinely serving its students. A school that posts strong proficiency numbers but quietly loses a quarter of its student body to chronic absence is not fully succeeding.

What the Research Says Actually Works

What are the most effective strategies for reducing chronic absenteeism in charter schools?

The evidence — and the results from schools actually moving the needle — consistently points to the same set of moves. Build a school culture where every student feels genuinely known and valued. Use daily attendance data to trigger fast, individualized outreach. Identify and remove practical barriers for families through community partnerships. Make instruction engaging enough that students have an intrinsic reason to show up. And abandon punitive approaches: involving law enforcement or using suspension as a response to absences fails to engage students or address root causes, and the research shows it produces negative long-term effects on attendance.

The schools making the most progress are the ones that have stopped treating attendance as a compliance problem and started treating it as a belonging problem. That shift in framing sounds simple. In practice, it changes everything about how a school is structured, how staff are trained, and how families are engaged from the very first day of the year.

---

Attendance is a leading indicator of almost everything else that matters in a student's educational journey. A student who shows up — whether in a classroom, in an online session, or through a sustained relationship with a teacher who genuinely checks in — is a student who can learn. The charter schools finding ways to keep that connection alive, regardless of what "showing up" looks like for each family, are pointing the rest of K–12 education toward what real post-pandemic recovery can look like.
