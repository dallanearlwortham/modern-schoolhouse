Charter Schools in the Age of ESAs: Where Do They Fit?

# Charter Schools in the Age of ESAs: Where Do They Fit?

The school choice movement has entered a new era — and it moved fast. As of March 2025, nine states had enacted universal Education Savings Accounts since 2021, and in 2025 alone, Idaho, Indiana, New Hampshire, Tennessee, Texas, and Wyoming all passed universal private school choice legislation. For families, the options on the table have never been broader. For charter schools, the question worth asking out loud is: what does all of this mean for us?

The short answer is that charter schools are not being crowded out — but they do need to understand their distinct value in a marketplace that has suddenly become a lot more competitive.

---

What's Actually Driving the ESA Surge

Private school choice is once again a major policy battleground in state legislatures, with lawmakers in multiple states pushing to expand publicly funded private school options through vouchers, Education Savings Accounts, and tax-credit scholarships. An ESA works differently from a traditional voucher: it is essentially a government-funded account that families can use not just for private school tuition, but also for school supplies, homeschooling curricula, tutoring, and other approved educational expenses — a vehicle that allows families to unbundle their child's education entirely.

Nearly half of all students participating in private school choice programs are now enrolled in ESAs, and five additional states are expected to add ESA programs by the end of 2026. At the federal level, President Trump issued an executive order in January 2025 instructing the Secretary of Education to release guidance on how states can use federal formula funds to support K-12 educational choice initiatives.

This is a genuine policy revolution — not a blip — and charter school leaders, families, and advocates need to understand how it changes the landscape they operate in.

---

Charter Schools Are Still the Largest Choice Sector

It would be easy to read those ESA headlines and assume charter schools are losing ground. The enrollment data tells a different story. Charter school enrollment grew by approximately 492,000 students between 2019–2020 and 2024–2025 — a 14.69% increase — and in 84% of states, charter school enrollment growth has outpaced growth in the overall school-aged population.

Even in states with robust, mature ESA programs, charters hold their own. In Arizona, which operates one of the country's most well-established universal ESA programs, charter school enrollment remains the highest of any school choice sector. The two models are not mutually exclusive: in some states, ESA funds can actually be used to access out-of-boundary charter schools, meaning ESAs can expand charter access for families who previously faced geographic or financial barriers.

The bottom line: ESAs are growing fast, but they are not eating charter schools' lunch.

---

What Makes Charter Schools Different — and Why It Still Matters

What exactly separates a charter school from a private school funded by an ESA? Charter schools are tuition-free public schools — funded by taxpayers and open to any student — that operate under a performance contract, their charter, with a public authorizer. Because they are public schools, they must be open to all students, publish outcome data, and answer to an authorizing body that can revoke their charter if they fail to perform. That accountability structure is a defining feature of the model, not a bureaucratic technicality.

Public charter schools arguably carry stronger accountability requirements than even traditional public schools, since their continued existence depends on meeting the terms of their charter. By contrast, many ESA-funded private schools operate largely outside the accountability systems that govern public dollars — with no mandated academic performance standards, no required transparency on outcomes, and no public authorizer watching the results.

For families who want a school with a verifiable track record, zero tuition, and a structured learning community, a charter school offers something an ESA-funded private arrangement simply cannot match: a publicly accountable commitment to results that anyone can look up.

---

The Question Families Are Actually Searching For

Is a charter school better than using an ESA? The honest answer depends on what a family prioritizes.

Families who want tuition-free enrollment, public accountability, a full-service school community, and the consistency of a traditional school calendar and campus will generally find a charter school to be the stronger fit. Families who want maximum flexibility — mixing homeschooling with private tutors, online courses, and curated electives — may be better served by an ESA. And increasingly, some families will use elements of both, particularly in states where ESA funds can be directed toward charter school enrollment or supplemental programs.

The key insight for charter school leaders is this: the ESA boom is not a threat to the charter model — it is an invitation to get much clearer about what makes charters uniquely valuable. A well-run charter school does not need to compete with an ESA on flexibility. It competes on community, consistency, mission, and outcome data that private operators are rarely required to publish.

---

How Charter Organizations Are Responding

Forward-thinking charter support organizations are already reframing their message for this new environment, recognizing that "school choice" now means something far more complex to families than it did five years ago. The Colorado League of Charter Schools is a strong example of this kind of proactive positioning: its mission centers explicitly on elevating charter schools as the forefront of public school choice — a framing that deliberately distinguishes the publicly accountable nature of charter education from the broader private choice marketplace. With work spanning legislative advocacy at the state capitol, school quality reviews, board governance support, enrollment communications, and the Great Schools Colorado initiative to support high-quality public school growth, the League is building the kind of sector-level infrastructure that helps charter schools stay competitive by doubling down on what makes them distinct — not by trying to out-flex private alternatives.

That kind of organized, sustained advocacy matters more than ever when families have more alternatives than at any point in American K-12 history.

---

What Charter Schools Should Be Communicating Right Now

As ESA programs continue to scale across the country, charter schools have a clear playbook for remaining the first choice for families who care about public accountability and community:

  • Lead with outcome data. Publish academic results prominently and make them easy to compare. Families choosing between a charter and an ESA-funded private school should be able to find the evidence quickly.
  • Own the tuition-free message. Charters are free by law. In a landscape where families are calculating how far a $7,000 ESA account will stretch toward private tuition, "free and high-performing" is a genuinely powerful proposition.
  • Sell the school community. Stable peer relationships, extracurriculars, counseling, and a consistent campus experience are things a curated collection of ESA vendors cannot easily replicate.
  • Lead with mission. Every charter school has a defined purpose baked into its founding documents. That clarity of mission is a differentiator in a crowded, confusing marketplace.
  • Show up in the choice conversation actively. Families are navigating open enrollment, ESAs, charter options, and inter-district transfers simultaneously. Charter schools that participate visibly in that conversation — through community events, local media, and family information nights — are the ones that earn trust before enrollment season begins.

---

The universal private school choice movement is expanding rapidly as it enters its fourth year in 2025–26, with more states launching programs, existing caps lifting, and state budgets for private choice growing in ways that would have seemed unlikely just a few years ago. That momentum is real and it is not reversing. But the charter school model — tuition-free, publicly accountable, mission-driven, and community-rooted — offers something the ESA marketplace cannot wholesale replicate. The schools and organizations that make that case clearly, consistently, and with solid evidence will not just survive this new era of school choice. They will define it.
