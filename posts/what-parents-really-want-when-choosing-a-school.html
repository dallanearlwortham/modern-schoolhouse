What Parents Really Want in a School (It's Not What You Think)

# What Parents Really Want in a School (It's Not What You Think)

Seventy-five percent of U.S. parents considered sending at least one child to a different school last year. That's roughly 46 million people quietly asking the same question: Is there something better out there for my kid?

According to a January 2026 survey of 3,960 parents by the National School Choice Awareness Foundation (NSCAF), school choice has shifted from a niche exception to a mainstream expectation for American families — and the data behind why families move (or don't) tells a far more nuanced story than the tired debate between "good schools" and "bad schools."

The Numbers Behind the Shift

In 2025, 75% of U.S. parents — about 46 million nationwide — considered, searched for, or enrolled at least one child in a new or different school, the highest level recorded in five years. But "considering" and "enrolling" are very different things. School searchers span the full decision spectrum: 23% of parents (about 14.5 million) considered new schools without searching further, 31% (19.6 million) actively searched but did not enroll, and 21% — roughly 13.3 million parents — ultimately enrolled a child in a new learning environment.

That gap between aspiration and action is one of the most important, and most underreported, findings in recent school choice research.

Charter school enrollment from 2020 to 2025 rose approximately 14.69%, reflecting a sustained shift in how families think about their options. Yet enrollment growth alone doesn't explain the full picture. Millions of families who want to switch never do. Understanding what holds them back — and what finally moves those who do act — is where the real insight lives.

Safety First. Academics Second. (Yes, Really.)

Ask most people why families choose charter or alternative schools and "better academics" will land near the top of every list. The data says something more complicated.

What do parents actually prioritize when choosing a new school? According to the NSCAF's January 2026 survey, parents who enrolled their children prioritized student well-being above all else, with 61% citing safety and a supportive environment. Social environment ranked next at 47%, followed by educational focus or theme at 39%, with strong academics cited by 36%.

That hierarchy holds up across other research. Three-fourths of parents rate "safety" as very important in their school-selection decisions — a notably larger proportion than those who rate academics (53%) and convenience (25%) as very important.

This doesn't mean parents don't care about academics. It means they're looking for a school where their child will be physically safe, emotionally supported, and socially connected — and they're betting, correctly, that academic achievement tends to follow from those conditions rather than precede them.

The Hidden Barrier: Belonging

One of the more striking recent findings comes from a fall 2025 survey conducted by Morning Consult on behalf of EdChoice and co-published by the Christensen Institute. It identified what researchers call an "aspiration-action gap" — the space between families who want to switch schools and those who actually follow through. For lower-income families, the dominant barrier is financial. For higher-income families, it's social: the cost of leaving behind community, friendships, and belonging built over years.

The research is direct about what this means: schools that invest in culture, community, and a genuine sense of belonging are building something that matters to families at every income level. The difference is that for lower-income families, those qualities are often the last thing addressed — and the first thing sacrificed when budgets are tight or logistics are hard.

In other words, the families who need strong school communities the most are often the ones least likely to find them, or to feel they can afford to leave their current school to go looking.

This reframes the school choice conversation entirely. It's not simply about academic options on a menu. It's about which schools have built something real enough that families feel the pull toward them — and something substantial enough that leaving carries a genuine cost.

What Charter Schools That Win Families Actually Offer

Parents often cite school environment, academic specialization, smaller class sizes, and innovative programs as reasons for choosing charter schools over their zoned public options. But the strongest charter schools aren't winning on any single dimension — they're winning because they've fused mission, culture, and academics into a coherent identity that families can feel when they walk in the door.

That coherence is increasingly what differentiates schools in a crowded choice landscape. A tuition-free K–8 college preparatory charter like Solare Collegiate Charter School in southwest Albuquerque illustrates the model well: its mission explicitly weaves together individualized support, character development, and academic rigor as a unified system aimed at equipping every student with the academic and personal skills to excel in the high school and college of their choice. The school's emphasis on six core values — explicitly taught and modeled by staff daily — is exactly the kind of intentional culture-building that research now shows families are weighing alongside (and often above) test scores when they decide where to enroll.

Why So Many Parents Search But Don't Switch

Understanding the "31% who searched but didn't enroll" is as strategically important as understanding those who did. The research points to several consistent friction points:

  • Financial barriers — even tuition-free public options carry hidden costs: transportation, uniforms, activity fees, and the loss of proximity to family support networks
  • Information gaps — Niche's 2025 survey found that 87% of parents researched schools on review sites before making contact or arranging a visit, meaning schools that aren't well-represented online are functionally invisible to families who are actively searching
  • Social inertia — for many families, especially higher-income ones, switching means leaving behind carpools, friendships, and community ties built over years
  • Uncertainty about fit — parents want concrete evidence of culture and belonging, not mission statements alone, before they'll uproot their child mid-journey

For charter schools specifically, this means the work of enrollment isn't just outreach and marketing. It's demonstrating — visibly, specifically, and repeatedly — that the community being built inside those walls is genuinely worth joining.

Practical Questions for Families Still on the Fence

If you're currently in the "considered but haven't moved" category, the research suggests a few honest questions worth sitting with before next enrollment season:

  1. What is your actual non-negotiable? Safety, academic mission, a particular philosophy, belonging? Naming it precisely makes the search faster and less paralyzing.
  2. Are you researching or just scrolling? Review sites are a starting point, not an endpoint. Visit schools in person. Ask current families what surprised them — good and bad.
  3. What is the real cost of staying? The bias toward inaction is powerful and often invisible. Make sure "doing nothing" is a deliberate choice, not just a default.

The Bigger Picture

School choice at its best isn't about escaping a bad situation. It's about finding the specific environment where a specific child can build something lasting — academic confidence, personal character, a sense of place. That's what the data, across multiple large surveys in 2025 and into 2026, consistently shows families are searching for.

And it turns out that's also exactly what the schools earning families' trust — charter and otherwise — are deliberately working to build. The schools winning in this moment aren't the ones with the best brochure. They're the ones where safety, belonging, and a clear academic mission aren't three separate selling points. They're the same thing.
